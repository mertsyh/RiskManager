Step 2: Problem & Population

Problem Statement
Approximately 70% of COM0463 student project charters contain risk registers with fewer than three risks and no probability or impact ratings. Roughly 60% of student teams report during retrospectives that they failed to monitor scope creep during the semester, discovering deviations only at final review. Abstract lecture-based instruction in risk identification and scope management produces insufficient internalization of iterative project monitoring behaviors.
Target Population
Year 3–4 Computer Engineering and Management Information Systems students enrolled in COM0463 (IT Project Management)

MDA Analysis
  • mechanics: Players interact with the Risk Card system to select PMBOK-aligned responses (Mitigate, Avoid, Transfer, Accept), manage a $100,000 budget, hire from 8 distinct WBS roles, and advance daily sprint cycles where progress is calculated using productivity, morale multipliers, and synergy bonuses.
  • dynamics: Aggressively accepting risks to save budget creates compounding failure states and bug events later in the project, while heavy investment in team morale and synergy pairs generates high-velocity productivity loops, forcing players to constantly balance short-term savings against long-term stability.
  • aesthetics: The simulation primarily evokes Challenge through tight budget and deadline constraints, while intense visual feedback like screen shakes, warning vignettes, and particle bursts create Sensation, making the abstract consequences of poor risk management emotionally impactful and memorable for the player.

Mechanics
  • Risk Card System: Displays 15 risk types as interactive cards with probability %, financial cost, schedule delay, and morale damage. Directly operationalises PMBOK Ch. 11 risk response planning.
  • Daily Sprint Simulation: Advance Day button triggers a sprint cycle calculating progress via employee productivity, energy, morale multiplier, and synergy bonuses. Mirrors Agile iteration loop.
  • Team Hiring and Synergy System: 8 roles available. 9 synergy pairs each add +8 progress/day when both are hired. Forces WBS-style role-to-risk-type decomposition.
  • Morale System: Morale decays 3 pts/day. Below 40% reduces productivity multiplier and triggers bugs. Above 70% boosts productivity and enables critical success rolls.
  • Technology Upgrade Shop: 6 permanent upgrades (e.g., CI/CD Pipeline, AI Copilot) that model PM infrastructure investment decisions and downstream velocity gains.
  • Office Dilemma System: 6 binary dilemmas trigger randomly from Day 3. Operationalises stakeholder communication and expectation management.

Step 5: Motivational Design

SDT — Autonomy support
Players freely choose the order and combination of risk responses, hiring decisions, and upgrade purchases with no prescribed optimal path. Multiple viable strategies all lead to viable outcomes, ensuring genuine decisional freedom.
SDT — Competence support
Difficulty escalates progressively: 3 starting risks → new risks spawn every 4 days → dilemmas introduce from Day 3. Visual effects (Critical Success ⭐ flash, 2× progress display) celebrate skill-consistent performance.
SDT — Relatedness support
Named characters (Alice, Bob, Diana…) with pixel art sprites, thought-bubble animations, and morale-driven expressions create parasocial connection. Firing a team member costs 12 morale points, enforcing responsibility.

Narrative Theme
Set in a high-stakes 2026 IT consultancy, 'Anthropic Ventures' challenges players to deliver a critical software project under tight constraints. The storyline empowers players using Self-Determination Theory by granting full autonomy over their risk and budget choices. The tension of the simulated office is directly driven by the Mechanics-Dynamics-Aesthetics framework, translating abstract PMBOK risk management into palpable narrative stakes. As the 30-day sprint advances, the character's intellectual journey explicitly mirrors the Bloom Taxonomy, pushing them from basic hazard identification to high-level strategic evaluation. The diverse strategic paths and milestone achievements appeal to various HEXAD user profiles, ensuring broad engagement. Ultimately, the narrative's success in training the Project Manager is validated through the Kirkpatrick four-level evaluation, bridging the gap between classroom fiction and real-world project delivery.

Why (Motivation)
Students consistently underperform on risk management assignments because they engage with risk as an abstract checklist. The game creates emotional memory of risk consequences through visceral visual feedback (red vignette, screen shake, budget flash), which is more pedagogically durable.
How (Ethics)
The simulation employs a strict compassion model. No personal data is stored, and failure states are purely instructional. The design enforces losePoints=0 to maintain a zero loss environment, ensuring students face no punitive academic measures for in-game budget or schedule miscalculations.
